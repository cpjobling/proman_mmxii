p-2011-004 – Matlab Demonstrations for Electrical Engineering

Set by Dr Christopher Jobling ( c.p.jobling@swansea.ac.uk ), Multidisciplinary Nanotechnology Centre ( MNC ).

Description

The Head of Teaching is keen to exploit technology to improve the range and scope of experiences available to students in Engineering. One of his ideas is to develop a library of examples in Matlab and/or Mathematica that can be used to demonstrate important engineering principles in lectures and could serve as tutorial problems. Electrical engineering is already a subject that is well served by such examples. The aim of this project would be survey the examples already available from the Mathworks and Wolfram and, with the help of lecture staff, identify new topics that can be illustrated in similar ways. It is expected that the student will also develop some demonstrations as well. Some example topics are signals and systems theory, control systems, modulation in communications, circuit modelling and simulation, three-phase and power systems, electromagnetism and antennas. An important requirement will be the exploitation of the user interface features provided by Matlab and/or Mathematica to create easy to use and compelling simulations. The resulting library will need to be well documented, usable by both staff and students, and easy to extend in the future. Examples that can be delivered on mobile devices such as smart phones or table PCs will be of particular interest. As inspiration for these see the Mobile and Tablet Apps page at Wolfram|Alpha http://products.wolframalpha.com/mobile/.

Special requirements

Personal access to Matlab and or Mathematica. Available via teaching licenses.

p-2011-005 – Exploiting Sensors in Mobile Phones

Set by Dr Pavel Loskot ( p.loskot@swansea.ac.uk ), Multidisciplinary Nanotechnology Centre ( MNC ).

Description

The widespread use of mobile phones creates opportunities for collection of various data about the environment. Thus, every mobile phone can be equipped with one or more sensors to obtain measurements from its immediate surroundings. For example, such measurements allow to map the local weather (barometric pressure, temperate and air humidity) or monitor the air quality. This student project will specify new applications that exploit data provided by different kind of sensors in the mobile phones. The specification will consider the associated signal processing in the data fusion centres and also take into account practical design constraints such as the cost of the sensors, the selective geographical and time availability of measurements, varying accuracy and reliability of the measurements, possible privacy issues, increased energy consumption in the mobile phones etc.

Special requirements

No special requirements.

p-2011-165 – Investigation of next-generation optical access network architectures

Set by Dr Joseph Kim ( k.s.kim@swansea.ac.uk ), Multidisciplinary Nanotechnology Centre ( MNC ).

Description

Fibre-To-The-Home (FTTH) has been considered an ideal solution for access networks due to its huge capacity. To practically and economically implement FTTH in access, Passive Optical Networks (PONs) – based on Time Division Multiplexing, Wavelength Division Multiplexing (WDM), or hybrid TDM/WDM – have been proposed and extensively studied in the literature. The aim of this project is to study various PON architectures proposed for next-generation optical access and evaluate their performances under realistic network and traffic environments using OMNeT++/INET, an open-source, object-oriented simulation framework.

Resources required: Access to computer and OMNeT++/INET.

p-2011-218 – Development of silicon nanowire sensors

Set by Dr Owen Guy ( o.j.guy@swansea.ac.uk ), Multidisciplinary Nanotechnology Centre ( MNC ).

Description

The development of miniaturised integrated systems for detection of disease biomarkers, at clinically relevant concentrations in biological samples, and drug delivery for application in early diagnosis, monitoring and treatment of diseases.

“Bio-chip” sensors, developed using semiconductor devices combined with micro-fluidics will enable detection of biomarkers at ultra-low concentrations.

This project will develop an integrated biosensor and drug delivery system. The sensor will be based on a “bioreceptor”-functionalised silicon nanowire (SiNW) capable of detecting biomarkers in blood, urine or even breath. The biosensor consists of a molecule attached to the SiNW surface, which is capable of interacting selectively with a specific target biomarker. This binding event between the charged biomarker and the bioreceptor produces a change in the charge density at the nanochannel / nanowire surface, that can be detected as a change in the electrical behaviour of the sensor device (essentially, a depletion of the nanochannel resulting in a change in the channel resistance).

p-2011-301 – gas sensing with ZnO nanostructures

Set by Dr Thierry Maffeis ( t.g.g.maffeis@swansea.ac.uk ), Multidisciplinary Nanotechnology Centre ( MNC ).

Description

Gas sensors based on polycrystalline metal oxide semiconductors offer many advantages over current technologies for detecting reducing gases (H2, CO, CH4) , such as low cost, long lifetime and high sensitivity. The sensing mechanism is based on changes in conductivity of the sensing film induced by chemical reactions between target gases and oxygen species adsorbed onto the film surface. Sensors usually operate at elevated temperature to promote chemical reactions. Additionally, sensors made with nano-sized particles exhibit a much higher sensitivity compared with micron sized particles. In this project, sensing devices will be fabricated using ZnO nanostructures such as nanocrystals and nanowires. The project will involve measuring the conductivity response of the devices as a function of their temperature and gaseous environment (vacuum, air, inert atmosphere and target gases) using a Keithley pico-Ammeter. Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) will also be used to characterise the nanoscale structure of the sensing device. SEM allows imaging down to a resolution of 1 nanometre.

p-2011-303 – electrical characterisation of ZnO nanostructures

Set by Dr Thierry Maffeis ( t.g.g.maffeis@swansea.ac.uk ), Multidisciplinary Nanotechnology Centre ( MNC ).

Description

The aim of this project is to optimize the growth parameters of ZnO nanostructures by the hydrothermal method. ZnO nanoparticles are currently used in the paints and cosmetics industry. Novel ZnO nanostructures such as nanowires have many potential applications and are ideal building blocks for nanoscale devices. Because of their small size, they have novel and exciting properties that can be harnessed into these nano-devices, with potential applications in many areas such as gas sensing, bio-sensing, nanomedicine, electronics and optoelectronics. During this project, the student will use a Keithley picoAmmeter and LabView to measure the electrical properties of the ZnO nanostructures in different configurations (electrical contacts, device geometry) and environments (vacuum, synthetic air, nitrogen atmosphere). Scanning electron microscopy will be used to characterise the nano-devices.
