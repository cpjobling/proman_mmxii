p-2011-015 – A cross section analysis of steel usage in the contruction sector of the less developed countries

Set by Dr Mark Evans ( m.evans@swansea.ac.uk ), Materials Research Centre ( MRC ).

Description

This project will developed a simple cross section model of steel usage in the less developed countries, based on the presumption that the inverted U-shaped intensity of use curve is shifted downward over time as a result of steel saving new technology, materials substitution and other time related factors. The model will be used to determine what rate of economic development is required in less developed countries to ensure growth in steel usage within the construction sectors of these economies. This project will involve collecting time series data and undertaking simple trend type analysis.

p-2011-016 – Electric Arc Steel making and the Market for Scrap Steel

Set by Dr Mark Evans ( m.evans@swansea.ac.uk ), Materials Research Centre ( MRC ).

Description

Since the early 1950’s minimill technologies have diffused to become part of the current steel making climate. For example, the share of electric arc steel production in total world crude steel production has risen steadily from the lows of 15% in 1970 to around 35% in 2002. This trend has had profound implications for the structure of the steel industry around the world. The continuation of this trend will also have significant implications for iron ore and coking coal demand in the longer term.

In this project you will research the reasons for this growth in electric arc steel manufacturing and assess the implication for the future of the large integrated plant. You will also build a model to forecast both steel scrap prices and the future growth of electric arc steel making and derive forecasts up to the year 2015.

p-2011-021 – The wave-induced drift velocity of a floating object

Set by Dr Chenfeng Li ( c.f.li@swansea.ac.uk ), Civil and Computational Engineering Centre ( C2EC ).

Description

When the wind blows, a floating object will experience a drag force in the direction of the wind. As the object is induced to drift through the water it will experience a hydrodynamic drag force in the opposite direction. Equilibrium results when the wind-induced aerodynamic drag force is balanced by the hydrodynamic drag force. This occurs at the drift velocity. The ratio of the drift velocity to the wind velocity will depend upon the proportion of the object above and below the waterline.

This project will deal specifically with the induced drift velocity for a floating human body. The work is required for forensic analysis in a criminal investigation. In the first stage, the project will take an experimental approach using the wind tunnel and the water tank available in the School and aim to provide a quick but rough estimation of the drift velocity. In the second stage, a computer simulation approach will be taken (using ANSYS CFX, a commercial computational fluid dynamics package) to confirm the experimental result and obtain more detailed prediction.

The project student is expected to have good knowledge in fluid mechanics, strong ability in experimental investigation, and an interest and open mind of computer simulation.

p-2011-023 – Finite element analysis of wheel chairs

Set by Dr Chenfeng Li ( c.f.li@swansea.ac.uk ), Civil and Computational Engineering Centre ( C2EC ).

Description

The wheelchair is an essential tool for immobile people who wish to travel indoors and out. A reliable wheelchair is essential for accessibility and confidence for the user, failure of the wheelchair frame could cause serious injury and discomfort for the user. A specific problem recognized with wheelchair failure is fatigue fracture in the cross-brace. It is a problem common with manual folding frame wheelchairs. Fatigue failure causes the chair to be inoperable for the user and could cause serious injury depending on the consequences after the failure. In order to avoid critical failures in wheelchair frames, standards are set for manufacturers to follow, theses involve static strength tests and fatigue tests.

This project will investigate the use of finite element analysis to predict the fatigue strength of a wheelchair frame. Due to the inconsistent nature of fatigue many factors are to be considered when assigning a fatigue strength property. The computer simulation will involve both both static and dynamic analysis. The commercial finite element analysis package “ANSYS” will be used as the main tool in the project.

The project student is required to have solid knowledge in both mechanics and mathematics, with strong motivation to work with real engineering problems. From the project, the student will gain knowledge of using computer simulation to assist analysis and design of complicated engineering structures and receive training of combining techniques to solve real engineering challenges.

p-2011-039 – Form-finding analysis in the design of prestressed structural membranes

Set by Dr Antonio Gil ( a.j.gil@swansea.ac.uk ), Civil and Computational Engineering Centre ( C2EC ).

Description

A prestressed membrane structure is a structural form which, due to its own geometrical shape, boundary conditions (supports) and prestressing loads, is subjected only to in-plane tensile forces, thus compressive/shear forces and bending/torsional moments are not present. Tensile membrane structures (possibly in combination with cable nets) are most often used as roofs as they can economically and attractively span large distances (i.e. the O2 Arena in London or the Olympic Stadium in Munich). Form-finding is the process by which an initial shape is obtained in equilibrium with the prestress pattern, prior to the application of in-service loads (i.e. snow, wind). In this project, various form-finding techniques will be investigated, in the context of cable-nets and tensile membranes, and comparisons will be drawn between them in terms of the resulting final shape.

Cross disciplinary research theme(s): Energy and environment

p-2011-041 – Analysis of Beam Torsion

Set by Prof Kenneth Morgan ( k.morgan@swansea.ac.uk ), Civil and Computational Engineering Centre ( C2EC ).

Description

Torsion in beams generally arises from the action of shear loads whose point of application does not coincide with the shear centre of the beam section. Codes of Practice require torsional stresses be checked and provided for in design. For general beams, exact solutions of torsion problems normally do not exist and approximate, numerical, methods of analysis must be employed. In this project, we will consider a beams of rectangular cross section, and begin by obtaining an exact solution via a separation of variables method. This will enable us to determine the value of the torsional rigidity of the beam. We will then solve the same problem using MATLAB and a basic finite difference method. The accuracy of the approximation will be demonstrated by comparing the computed and exact values of the torsional rigidity. To finish, we will use a higher order finite difference method and demonstrate its improved accuracy characteristics.

Special requirements

Student access to MATLAB

p-2011-045 – Analysis of Water Hammer Problems

Set by Prof Kenneth Morgan ( k.morgan@swansea.ac.uk ), Civil and Computational Engineering Centre ( C2EC ).

Description

Water hammer is a pressure surge resulting when water in motion is forced to stop or change direction suddenly. At home, water hammer sometime occurs occur when a tap shuts off water flow, causing a loud repetitive banging or shuddering. The process is often simulated using the method of characteristics. We will start by looking at the water hammer problem and will produce a one dimensional model in the form of a coupled pair of equations. We will then look at the method of characteristics and use it initially for a single equation. The extension of the method to handle two coupled equations will then be addressed. The method of characteristics will then be applied to the water hammer equations and an approximate MATLAB implementation will be developed. This will then enable simulations to be performed of the flows resulting from the closure of a downstream valve or a change in pipe cross-section or a pipe branch.

Special requirements

Student access to MATLAB

p-2011-056 – Uncertainty quantification in computational modelling

Set by Dr Paul Ledger ( p.d.ledger@swansea.ac.uk ), Civil and Computational Engineering Centre ( C2EC ).

Description

In engineering computational modelling using finite elements, finite difference or other numerical methods is routinely undertaken to get approximate solutions of complex engineering problems. Unfortunately, however, the results of the computational simulations may contain several elements that lead to uncertainties as to regards the results obtained. Aspects of uncertainty in computational modelling include

• Doubts over whether the mathematical model or equations are correct • Doubts over the input data (geometry, material parameters etc) • Doubts over the computational model • Doubts over the accuracy of the computational simulations

The first part of this project will include a literature survey to identify how these areas of uncertainty arise and the computational approaches that are existence to control them or quantify them. In the second part of the project the student will use existing finite element software to test out different refinement strategies to understand how best to control the interpolation error.

p-2011-069 – Smashing Fake Egyptian Relics

Set by Dr Robert Bryant ( r.bryant@swansea.ac.uk ), Multidisciplinary Nanotechnology Centre ( MNC ).

Description

Fragments of ancient Egyptian earthenware figurines of cobras have been found and there are suggestions that these may have been destroyed as part of ritual activity. It is also possible that they have been destroyed by natural and accidental processes. The question arises as to whether fragments arise from ritual activity or the latter. A previous project has examined the effects of dropping and crushing symmetrical items of consistent quality and has reported the statistical distributions arising from replicate tests. A batch of figurines has been commissioned to replicate those from ancient Egypt and these will have been produced in a manner typical of that likely to have been used in ancient Egypt (i.e. without the quality control of mass production). It is proposed that a series of dropping and crushing tests be performed on these asymmetrical objects. (No in-tact ancient figurines are available for the tests.) The project involves performing the tests and analyzing the outcomes in relation to the previous study. The aim is to determine whether the deliberate ritualistic destruction of such figurines is detectable.

Direct time integration is used widely for the solution of large deformation problems in solid and structural mechanics.
