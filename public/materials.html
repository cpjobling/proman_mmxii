p-2011-017 – Phenomenological of High Temperature Deformation Data for 1Cr-1Mo Steel

Set by Dr Mark Evans ( m.evans@swansea.ac.uk ), Materials Research Centre ( MRC ).

Description

When designing materials for high temperature service, the design criteria for long term operation must guarantee that creep deformation should not cause excessive distortion over the planned service life and that creep failure should not occur within such a required operating life. Such creep fracture represents an obvious ‘life limiting’ design consideration as fracture of pipe work or other major components used by nuclear powered electricity generating plants could prove catastrophic. For this reason, studying their ability to predict the time to rupture strain has been the major criteria used to assess creep extrapolation techniques. One of the major steels used in pipe work in power generation plants is 1Cr-1Mo-0.25V. This project will compare the accuracy of the new “Wilshire” equations with some more traditional parametric techniques in predicting the life of these steels.

p-2011-164 – Grain refinining Al-alloys using high-intensity ultrasound

Set by Dr Amitabha Das ( a.das@swansea.ac.uk ), Materials Research Centre ( MRC ).

Description

Due to their low density and good environmental stability Al-alloys are increasingly being considered as the preferred structural material replacing steel. This trend is likely to increase further in the automotive sector in order to reduce car weight, fuel consumption and achieve lower CO2 emissions. Despite their superior specific strength, Al-alloys have only been used in prestige cars due to the high processing cost and relatively lower strength. In order to achieve widespread Al usage in consumer vehicles, strength and cost reduction has become essential. Both mechanical strength and solid state processability (thus, reduced processing cost) can be improved by grain refinement. Although chemical grain refinement is now a standard industrial practice for Al, it has limitations such as unused inoculant particles severely reduce the fatigue performance (major concern for aerospace and automotive usage). Most importantly, chemical inoculation only works for a handful of alloys and is mostly ineffective in many commercially important Alloys. Current research has demonstrated that use of high-energy ultrasound during solidification can lead to dramatic microstructural refinement in a wide range of light alloys. This promising technology can lead to a physical grain refinement mechanism that can address the limitations of conventional techniques and lead to Al-alloys with better property, superior fatigue performance and universal applicability irrespective of alloy additions. In this work the effectiveness of high-intensity ultrasonic irradiation on grain refinement will be explored in various Al-alloys.

Cross disciplinary research theme(s): Aerospace and manufacturing

p-2011-176 – A new instrumental technique for visualising latent fingerprints

Set by Dr Geraint Williams ( geraint.williams@swansea.ac.uk ), Materials Research Centre ( MRC ).

Description

Latent fingerprints on items of evidence in criminal investigations are typically visualised by the use of a developer, which increases the optical contrast between the fingerprint pattern and the background surface. Most routine print detection work is carried out using reagents such metal powders, various dyes and superglue. However, for metal surfaces it has been shown that an instrumental technique based on Volta potential mapping using a scanning Kelvin probe (SKP) generates superior results under certain circumstances. The principal aim of this work will be to determine whether or not SKP can visualise fingerprint patterns in instances where recovery of ridge pattern detail has not been satisfactory using various chemical developers. The sensitivity of SKP versus techniques such as superglue fuming and metal deposition will be determined using a fingerprint depletion series approach.

Special requirements

Resources required: Chemical reagents (including palladium chloride) (~£50) and metal samples.

p-2011-177 – Thin organic coatings for the protection of sheet steel products

Set by Dr Geraint Williams ( geraint.williams@swansea.ac.uk ), Materials Research Centre ( MRC ).

Description

In order to maintain a strategic advantage over foreign competitors in coated product technology, the UK steel industry is currently developing high performance organic coatings with minimal environmental impact. Thin organic coatings (TOCs) are presently being investigated as one-step alternatives to using costly combinations of pre-treatments, conversion coatings and organic polymer based primer layers which contain anti-corrosion pigments. TOCs are intended for use on steel prior to the application of a topcoat and must confer a level of corrosion protection equivalent to existing technologies. The aim of this project is to identify promising coating systems for use as TOCs at thicknesses of 5 microns or less, along with appropriate methods of application. The most promising candidates will be used to coat relevant metal substrates on a laboratory scale and will be assessed with respect to corrosion resistance using a combination of time-lapse photography and in-situ scanning Kelvin probe analysis. It should be noted that this work will be carried out within the SPECIFIC laboratories in Baglan and that the student should have his or her own transport.

Special requirements

Resources required: Access to SKP/elctrochemistry equipment, time-lapse photography. Lab consumables (~£50).

p-2011-267 – Dye-sensitized solar cells – from rigid to flexible

Set by Prof David Worsley ( d.a.worsley@swansea.ac.uk ), Materials Research Centre ( MRC ).

Description

Dye-sensitized solar cells (DSC) have often been described as artificial photosynthesis. A DSC generally consists of a mesoporous TiO layer with a sunlight absorbing dye (much like chlorophyll in green leaves) adsorbed to its surface deposited onto a conducting substrate. An electrolyte solution surrounding the TiO2 and connecting it to a platinised counter electrode completes the photovoltaic circuit. The project will entail developing an understanding into the effects of transferring conventional rigid glass based dye-sensitized solar cell technology onto flexible substrates and any photovoltaic losses. The project will involve the manufacture of rigid and flexible dye-sensitized solar cells on glass and titanium substrates and their characterisation with a particular interest on the substrate - TiO2 interface. This project will be embedded in the SPECIFIC centre within a larger activity aimed at developing energy generating functional coatings for the built environment.

Special requirements

This project will be based in the labs at the SPECIFIC Innovation and Knowledge Centre in Baglan. Successful students should co-ordinate their travel plans

Polymers subjected to a combination of stress and a hostile environment may exhibit Environmental Stress Cracking (ESC), depending on the precise conditions. This project should aim to investigate these conditions concentrating on a limited combination of material and environments. p-2011-366 – Finite Element Model Updating using Response Surface Method

Set by Dr Rajib Chowdhury ( r.chowdhury@swansea.ac.uk ), Materials Research Centre ( MRC ).

Description

This project proposes the response surface method for finite element model updating. The response surface method is implemented by approximating the finite element model surface response equation by adaptive high dimensional model representation (HDMR). The updated parameters of the finite element model were calculated using genetic algorithm by optimizing the surface response equation. The proposed method will then be compared with the existing methods that use simulated annealing or genetic algorithm together with a full finite element model for finite element model updating. The proposed method will be tested on an aircraft wing structure. It is anticipated that the proposed method will provide the updated natural frequencies and mode shapes that were of the same order of accuracy as those given by simulated annealing and genetic algorithm. Furthermore, the proposed approach would provide an efficient computational tool compared with the genetic algorithm and the simulated annealing.

Special requirements

Matlab, finite element software (ANSYS)

p-2011-367 – Atomistic simulation of fracture of copper symmetric tilt grain boundary

Set by Dr Rajib Chowdhury ( r.chowdhury@swansea.ac.uk ), Materials Research Centre ( MRC ).

Description

This project aims to perform an atomistic simulation of fracture of copper symmetric tilt grain boundary. A parallel molecular dynamics code, LAMMPS, will be used to calculate stresses at the grain boundary as the strain of the bicrystal is incrementally increased. Matlab will be used to plot a stress-strain curve, and Visual Molecular Dynamics (VMD) will be used to visualize the simulation.

This molecular dynamics simulation calculates the stress-strain relationship of copper symmetric tilt grain boundary under fracture. The grain boundary structure in this case is a <100> Σ5(210) symmetric tilt grain boundary.. The simulation cell is defined such that the bicrystal is pulled in the y-direction, or perpendicular to the boundary interface, to increase strain. The strain in increased for a specified number of times in a loop, and the stress is calculated at each point before the simulation loops.

Special requirements

LAMMPS, Matlab, VMD
