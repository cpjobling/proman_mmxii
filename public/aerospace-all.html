p-2011-057 – Implementation of a panel method for lifting airfoils

Set by Dr Paul Ledger ( p.d.ledger@swansea.ac.uk ), Civil and Computational Engineering Centre ( C2EC ).

Available for selection in next round? No

This project has been allocated to student with id: 554339

Description

Panel methods are part of a more general class of numerical method for the solution of incompressible flow past airfoils. They have the advantage that these models only require discretisation of the geometry and do not require the discretisation of the flow body. Other numerical methods for incompressible irrotational flow require a insertion of a cut to correcting model lift, but panel methods do not.

This would serve as useful introduction to a technique that is widely used in the Aerospace Industry.

p-2011-140 – The effect of processing method on the mechanical response of titanium alloys

Set by Dr Mark Whittaker ( m.t.whittaker@swansea.ac.uk ), Materials Research Centre ( MRC ).

Available for selection in next round? No

Description

The hexagonal close packed nature of the alpha phase titanium lattice leads to the development of a strong crystallographic texture within a number of titanium alloys. This may lead to anisotropic mechanical properties which require full evaluation prior to implementation in engineering applications. Currently there is much focus on the development of ‘macrozones’, areas of common crystallographic orientation which may have a further detrimental effect on mechanical properties. The student will be required to work alongside existing researchers in analysing material behaviour from a number of different microstructural titanium variants. The ultimate aim of the project is the development of understanding of the behaviour of these macrozones, which would lead to an overall improvement in properties which could be beneficial to the aerospace and other sectors.

Cross disciplinary research theme(s): Aerospace and manufacturing

p-2011-141 – Assessment of creep lifing methods in aerospace

Set by Dr Mark Whittaker ( m.t.whittaker@swansea.ac.uk ), Materials Research Centre ( MRC ).

Available for selection in next round? No

Description

The recently developed Wilshire equations have shown great promise in the creep lifing of a number of different alloys, particularly in the prediction of long term creep behavior based on short term predictions. However, the creep lifing requirements for the aerospace sector differ significantly from the long term nature of the power industry. More important here is the accurate prediction of times to particular strains. Based on these results it has been shown that it is possible to regenerate full creep curves based on limited data. Clearly the Wilshire equations offer exciting new opportunities for the field of creep research. The role of the student in this project will be to work alongside existing researchers in further developing the Wilshire equations for use in the aerospace sector.

Cross disciplinary research theme(s): Aerospace and manufacturing

p-2011-162 – Elastomer Skin for Morphing Aircraft

Set by Prof Michael Friswell ( m.i.friswell@swansea.ac.uk ), Civil and Computational Engineering Centre ( C2EC ).

Available for selection in next round? No

Description

A variety of designs have been suggested for morphing wings, including compliant ribs, span extension and morphing winglets. These concepts usually require a skin that is flexible, so that the wing can deform, and stiff to withstand the aerodynamic loads. One option to satisfy these conflicting requirements is to employ skins with anisotropic properties, such as corrugated skins or stiffened elastomer skins. This project will consider the design of an elastomer skin to cover a honeycomb wing structure. The direction on the project will, to some extent, be dictated by the interests and background of the student, but is likely to include modelling of the skin deformation and actuator force requirements, the design of suitable skins for typical applications and/or manufacturing and testing stiffened elastomer skins.

Cross disciplinary research theme(s): Aerospace and manufacturing

p-2011-196 – Mass Uncertainty in Aerospace Structures

Set by Prof Sondipon Adhikari ( s.adhikari@swansea.ac.uk ), Civil and Computational Engineering Centre ( C2EC ).

Available for selection in next round? No

This project has been allocated to student with id: 487056

Description

The description of complex aircraft structures is inevitably associated with some amount of uncertainty in specifying mass. The uncertainty of mass arises from variable cargos and passengers which are generally not known precisely during the design. The aim of the project is (a) to quantify the effect of mass uncertainty on the vibration response of the system, and (b) to validate and update the numerical (finite element) model in the light of such uncertainties. Vibration analysis will be conducted on simplified structures (for example, a cantilever beam and plate). Uncertainty will be simulated using randomly placed lightweight spring-mass oscillators on these structures. Statistical analysis of the response will be compared against finite element models with uncertain parameters. This project will result enhanced understanding of uncertainty quantification and model validation essential for future generation of aircraft design.

p-2011-278 – High Temperature Deformation Characteristics of an Advanced Nickel Based Superalloy

Set by Dr Karen Perkins ( k.m.perkins@swansea.ac.uk ), Materials Research Centre ( MRC ).

Available for selection in next round? No

This project has been allocated to student with id: 557454

Description

Description: If the aerospace industry is to achieve its aim in reducing emissions and impact on the environment, the next generation of jet engines will need to burn fuel more efficiently and at higher temperatures. Building engine components capable of operating under such harsh environments is a technological challenge and while nickel based superalloys offer the heat resistance needed, they are difficult to join using conventional welding techniques. Rolls Royce are tackling this problem by applying inertia welding techniques to the production of compressor drums, turbine discs and shafts for jet engines. Inertia welding uses the heat generated by friction to fuse metal components together. A work piece is spun at high speed on a flywheel and brought into contact with a stationary component. Within seconds, the pieces reach forging temperature at the point of contact and are bonded together without melting or the addition of liquid metal. Rolls Royce are developing a model to simulate this process in the rotor disc alloy, RR1000 in order to optimize the welding process both in terms of structural integrity and to predict/ minimize residual stresses. In order for the model to make accurate predictions, mechanical property data is required as an input, in particular flow stress information obtained from forging tests. The project will involve data analysis for a range of temperatures and strain rates and the development of a curve fitting/ interpolation program. This program should be able to predict the flow stress for a given temperature and strain rate. Ideally the fitting routine should utilise a phenomenological deformation model. Material characterization will also form part of the analysis in order to define failure mechanisms and material stability within the defined test parameters.

Special requirements

basic metallographic consumables – 12hours SEM/microspcopy

p-2011-279 – Characterisation of corrosion-fatigue damage in an advanced nickel based superalloy

Set by Dr Karen Perkins ( k.m.perkins@swansea.ac.uk ), Materials Research Centre ( MRC ).

Available for selection in next round? No

This project has been allocated to student with id: 492077

Description

Description: The latest generation of nickel based superalloys must offer improved temperature capabilty to aero-engine and land based energy turbine manufacturers in order to improve plant effeciency and meet increasingly stringent envronmental regulations. Due to the combination of alloy metallurgy and extreme operational conditions, such alloys are prone to high temperature oxidation and sulphidation damage that can potentially compromise subsequent mechanical performance.

This short project will characterise surface damage incurred during normal service operation in turbine disc components supplied by Rolls-Royce. As operating conditions (especially temperature) are pushed beyond previously defined design limits, instances of high temperature corrosion are becoming more regular. However, to date the scientific understanding of such damage remains extremely limited. An assessment of ex-service parts is now considered essential since fatigue cracks could initiate from sites of surface corrosion, acting as localised stress raising features (e.g. oxidised grain boundaries and surface inclusions, sulphidation pitting etc). Thus, the project will have direct engineering implications together with academic interest. The project objectives can be summarised as: 1. Detect instances of surface oxidation / sulphidation in ex-service turbine components 2. Characterise typical forms of high temperature corrosion damage 3. Create an imaging database to archive instances of such damage 4. Define physical corrosion metrics (corroded area, pit depths etc) 5. Consider the potential implications for mechanical performance (fatigue and creep)

Special requirements

Basic metallographic consumables, SEM/microscopy 12 hours

p-2011-280 – Small Scale test techniques in linear friction weld development

Set by Dr Karen Perkins ( k.m.perkins@swansea.ac.uk ), Materials Research Centre ( MRC ).

Available for selection in next round? No

This project has been allocated to student with id: 487480

Description

Industry in general is faced with substantial challenges in the field of structural metals if it is to secure future sustainable growth. In order to meet demanding performance and environmental targets a considerable drive to improve upon conventional metallic alloys will be necessary. It is widely considered that improvements to traditional materials via composition optimization alone have been exhausted , therefore, future engineering designs are likely to incorporate innovative processing techniques and ‘hybrid’ components employing dissimilar combinations of two or more alloy variants. Key to achieving this will be the development of novel joining technologies. The capability to join a metal to itself or to a different alloy greatly increases flexibility in design, production and repair when compared with traditional processing routes such as casting or forging and consequently, a wide variety of different joining techniques have been developed. Solid state welding techniques, such as linear friction welding are receiving considerable interest within the aerospace industry in the joining of advanced alloy systems, where these alloys are only joinable via such friction weld based processes

This project will underpin the development of friction welding methods and process models for friction welding of titanium, nickel and steel alloys. The work will develop and understand a small scale disc bend test method, in order to characterize the mechanical properties of development linear friction welds. Carry out a range of tests to build up a database of results to ensure validity of the method and its relevant equations. The technique will be particularly effective when looking into weld simulations on φ10mm cylindrical rods, where the heat/cool treated zone is of a small thickness. Exploration of different heat profiles with the aim to establish effects on microstructure, properties and in turn optimising the process.

Special requirements

basic metallographic consumables – 12hours SEM/microspcopy

Description

Trusses are widely used in aerospace, mechanical and civil engineering. Optimal design of these trusses can be done using performance criteria, such as stiffness, strength and weight. p-2011-399 – Study of mesh sensitivity to the computation of the wall shear stress by the finite element method

Set by Dr Igor Sazanov ( i.sazonov@swansea.ac.uk ), Civil and Computational Engineering Centre ( C2EC ).

Available for selection in next round? No

Description

Computation of an unsteady flow in pipes is important for aerodynamic, civil, biomedical and other applications. This problem can be reduced to a numerical solving of a PDE in a 2D domain. It can be easily done by the finite element (FE) method using an unstructured mesh for the domain. The challenge is to prepare mesh enough to resolve velocity gradient just near the pipe wall. The student will be provided for the mesh generator and the FE solver. The task is to add structured boundary layer mesh and find its optimal parameters for computation of wall shear stress with the given accuracy. p-2011-401 – Computation of vibration insulations by a system of oscillators

Set by Dr Igor Sazanov ( i.sazonov@swansea.ac.uk ), Civil and Computational Engineering Centre ( C2EC ).

Available for selection in next round? No

Description

One of effective methods of vibro-insulation is setting a mechanical oscillator or system of such oscillator onto vibrating rod, beam, plate, shell etc. The challenge is to determine parameters of oscillators and their location to decrease vibration in a prescribed frequency range. The project includes the following stages:

  1. Study types of vibration in a rod or beam.
  2. Compute insulation of torsional vibration in a beam by a single oscillator.
  3. Compute insulation of torsional vibration in a beam by a system of identical oscillators uniformly distributed along the beam.
  4. In the case of few oscillators with different parameters, optimize their parameters to get maximal insulation in the given frequency range.

Special requirements

Access to School PCs and MATLAB is required.

p-2011-402 – Solving Eikonal equation for finding centrelines in a domain Description

Set by Dr Igor Sazanov ( i.sazonov@swansea.ac.uk ), Civil and Computational Engineering Centre ( C2EC ).

Available for selection in next round? No

Description

One of the problems when dealing with topologically complex objects is simplify its description determining the so-called centerline. This is also called a skelotonization of a domain. One of the approaches is to solve Eikonal equation. Its solution describes distance between the given point and the nearest boundary of the domain. Utilizing this solution one can compute positions of centerlines. The project includes the following stages:

  1. Study finite-difference methods for solving partial differential equations.
  2. Prepare a MATLAB code for solving the 2D Eikonal equation.
  3. Analysing the solution and finding the centreline.
  4. Prepare a code for automatic determination of centralines.

The project requires skills in mathematics, numerical methods and in MATLAB programming.

Special requirements

Access to School PCs and MATLAB is required.
