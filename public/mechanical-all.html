p-2011-004 – Matlab Demonstrations for Mechanical Engineering

Set by Dr Christopher Jobling ( c.p.jobling@swansea.ac.uk ), Multidisciplinary Nanotechnology Centre ( MNC ).

Available for selection in next round? No

Description

The Head of Teaching is keen to exploit technology to improve the range and scope of experiences available to students in Engineering. One of his ideas is to develop a library of examples in Matlab and/or Mathematica that can be used to demonstrate important engineering principles in lectures and could serve as tutorial problems. Mechanical engineering is already a subject that is well served by such examples. The aim of this project would be survey the examples already available from the Mathworks and Wolfram and, with the help of lecture staff, identify new topics that can be illustrated in similar ways. It is expected that the student will also develop some demonstrations as well. Some example topics are signals and systems theory, control systems, statics and dynamics and system modelling and simulation. An important requirement will be the exploitation of the user interface features provided by Matlab and/or Mathematica to create easy to use and compelling simulations. The resulting library will need to be well documented, usable by both staff and students, and easy to extend in the future. Examples that can be delivered on mobile devices such as smart phones or table PCs will be of particular interest. As inspiration for these see the Mobile and Tablet Apps page at Wolfram|Alpha http://products.wolframalpha.com/mobile/.

Special requirements

Personal access to Matlab and or Mathematica. Available via teaching licenses.

p-2011-018 – Machine Intelligence and the Formability of Tubular Blanks

Set by Dr Mark Evans ( m.evans@swansea.ac.uk ), Materials Research Centre ( MRC ).

Available for selection in next round? No

This project has been allocated to student with id: 556812

Description

Tube hydro forming has already proved successful in sub frame and chassis applications. Due to the fact that standard tube making methods are restricted to production of tubes with a diameter to thickness ratio of 65:1, when 100:1 is required, there has been limited uptake of this technology. However, the Corus tubular blank process allows a greater diameter to thickness ratio and so has more potential for wide spread use in Chassis applications. The major technical issue concerned with the manufacture of Corus tubular blanks is the effect of material elastic recovery (measured through springback) after forming has taken place.

This project will develop a Fuzzy Neural Network model (the so called ANFIS architecture) for springback prediction and in the process identify the important design and process variables associated with springback. The predictions from the model will then be compared to those obtained using existing predictive models. Finally, The finished Fuzzy Neural Network will be used to find settings for the process variables that help minimise elastic recovery.

p-2011-019 – The Effect of Process Variables on the Mechanical Properties of Titanium blades

Set by Dr Mark Evans ( m.evans@swansea.ac.uk ), Materials Research Centre ( MRC ).

Available for selection in next round? No

Description

The use of bladed discs has the potential to eliminate complex aerofoil attachment arrangements that can result in a substantial reduction in weight. For this project you will be presented with the results from a systematic study of the effect of process variables on the mechanical properties of friction welded Ti-64 aero engine blades. The objective of the project will then be to build a Fuzzy Neural Network model (the so called ANFIS architecture) model of this friction welding process. This will be compared to the results obtained using a response surface methodology. From these models you will seek those manufacturing conditions that maximise the degree of upset in the finished welds and minimise the variability in upset around this maximum.

p-2011-030 – Geometrically-Necessary Dislocation Accumulation at Grain Boundaries

Set by Prof Valerie Randle ( v.randle@swansea.ac.uk ), Materials Research Centre ( MRC ).

Available for selection in next round? No

Description

Geometrically-necessary dislocation (GND) content can be determined by the effect of dislocations on the lattice curvature of a crystal. Local orientations are measured using Electron Backscatter Diffraction (EBSD), which are then used to determine the local orientation gradients and resolved into the GND content. There is experimental evidence that GND content accumulates at grain boundaries during plastic deformation, the magnitude of which may be related to the geometry of the grain boundary. The objective of this project will be to determine any relationship between the GND content accumulated at a grain boundary and the grain boundary character in strained and annealed samples of copper. The student will use orientation data obtained using EBSD to create maps of GND content in the sample. The student will then use image analysis software to obtain plots of GND content as a function of distance from the boundary. Once a sufficient number of boundaries are measured in this manner, the data will be compiled according to the boundary geometry and character as well as the amount of induced strain. This project is also suitable for Materials or Mechanical students.

Special requirements

Electron Backscatter Diffraction, SEM, Induction Furnace, Metallographic Specimen Preparation, Data Analysis, Image Analysis Software

p-2011-031 – Relative grain boundary energies from dihedral angle measurements

Set by Prof Valerie Randle ( v.randle@swansea.ac.uk ), Materials Research Centre ( MRC ).

Available for selection in next round? No

Description

During long duration, low temperature anneals there is evidence that the dihedral angles around triple junctions can change, allowing the boundaries to lie in lower energy positions. The objective of this project is to devise a method to measure the change in these dihedral angles, for body-centred cubic metals, before and after long duration, low temperature anneals. Conclusions are to then be drawn from the observations in regard to change in dihedral angle and grain boundary energies. This project is also suitable for Materials or Mechanical students.

Special requirements

Electron Backscatter Diffraction, SEM, furnace, metallographic specimen preparation

p-2011-169 – Structure and property degradation in Welded Al-alloy joints

Set by Dr Amitabha Das ( a.das@swansea.ac.uk ), Materials Research Centre ( MRC ).

Available for selection in next round? No

This project has been allocated to student with id: 553522

Description

Al-alloys are increasingly being used to replace steel in many structural applications due to their good corrosion performance and better specific strength. The use of Al-alloys is expected to increase significantly in automotive applications to reduce vehicle weight, fuel consumption, and CO2 emission. Throughout the UK and EU, Al-alloys are being used to completely replace galvanised-steel street and motorway lighting columns. Welding is used as the most common joining method for Al-parts. However, the heat-affected region created due to welding may render some of these joints susceptible to failure in demanding applications where high stress and environment factors play a decisive role. This is more critical in heat treatable high strength alloys where the strength loss due to welding may be prevalent in the heat affected zones and failure may occur due to stress, corrosion or a combination of both. The aim of the project is to investigate the structure, property and corrosion behaviour of welded joints from heat treatable aluminium-alloys. The effect of designed thermal treatments on improving the joint properties will be explored if time permits.

Cross disciplinary research theme(s): Aerospace and manufacturing

p-2011-170 – Comparison of structure and mechanical property in magnesium alloys processed through convention high-pressure die casting and newly-developed rheodiecasting.

Set by Dr Amitabha Das ( a.das@swansea.ac.uk ), Materials Research Centre ( MRC ).

Available for selection in next round? No

This project has been allocated to student with id: 625331

Description

Magnesium is the lightest of all structural material and a promising candidate for aerospace, automotive and other structural applications due to their high specific strength and stiffness, good damping properties, good electromagnetic shielding and ease of machining. Advanced Mg-alloys are now being used in helicopters and it is anticipated that proper alloy and process development will significantly increase the use of Mg-alloys in cars. Magnesium car can very effectively address the necessary fuel and emission reduction as required by the ever stringent environment legislations. Drawback of magnesium alloys are low absolute strength, poor corrosion performance and manufacturing cost too prohibitive for mass application in the automotive industry. Many alloys have been developed with improved corrosion property and can be manufactured cost effectively through high-speed die casting. However, majority of the cast alloys are not designed to be heat treated to improve strength, and show poor mechanical property following processing. A new and promising development to address the shortcoming of Mg processing is the development of semisolid processing routes that promise to significantly improve and refine the cast structure, eliminate defects and improve mechanical property of the components processed in near-net shape. Such development can significantly reduce the cost of processing and mechanical performance of Mg-components by combining elements from casting and solid state processing together. In the present work, a systematic study will be undertaken to investigate and compare the structure and property of advanced magnesium alloy processed through conventional HPDC and semisolid HPDC. Based on the results the relative strengths and shortcomings of the new technology will be evaluated.

Cross disciplinary research theme(s): Aerospace and manufacturing

p-2011-188 – Chromate free coating systems for the protection of steels in the 21st century

Set by Dr James Sullivan ( j.h.sullivan@swansea.ac.uk ), Materials Research Centre ( MRC ).

Available for selection in next round? No

This project has been allocated to student with id: 575106

Description

A large percentage of steel is used in the construction industry and increasingly in the pre-painted form in structures such as the Millennium stadium in Cardiff, Supermarkets, DIY stores and large warehouses. Here the paint is applied by the steel maker and simply assembled onsite. These materials have long warranty periods of up to 40 years and failure generally occurs at the cut-edge of the material where metal is exposed and may corrode allowing the paint to peel off. To combat cut-edge corrosion the steels are coated with a zinc alloy and additions of corrosion inhibitors are made to the paint layers to retard corrosion. These inhibitors are critical and have traditionally been based on chromate systems. However, the chromium (VI) ions are carcinogenic and the use of such materials is being reduced through stringent legislation. Thus, steel companies are driven to find effective Chromate free systems that offer comparable corrosion resistance. This project will encompass a detailed literature survey of the current advances in chrome free technologies involving novel systems such as titanates, phosphates, ion exchange systems etc. to enable Tata steel to develop the coatings of the future.

p-2011-220 – Development of low-cost optical lenses for concentrated photovoltaics (CPV)

Set by Dr Owen Guy ( o.j.guy@swansea.ac.uk ), Multidisciplinary Nanotechnology Centre ( MNC ).

Available for selection in next round? No

Description

This project will develop novel production technology for low-cost Concentrator PV (CPV) using reclaimed silicon wafers from the semiconductor industry with low-cost concentrator lenses.

Low-cost photovoltaics (PV) will be the major sustainable, clean energy solution for future energy demands. Silicon dominates PV technology (80% PV market share) and PV technology roadmaps predict that silicon cells will continue to be the primary source of solar power.

Following the new government Feed-in Tariff (FIT) scheme, introduced to promote uptake of renewable electricity generation technologies, the demand for solar power is booming.

Standard silicon cells have good efficiencies but require high energy inputs during manufacturing. Silicon wafers, reclaimed from the semiconductor industry (ideal for PV cells), do not require any thermal processing. Recycled wafers thus represent the greenest possible silicon PV option with energy payback times reduced by more than 5 times. Innovative large area (250 mm sq.) cells from reclaimed wafers will be integrated with novel low-cost Concentrating Photovoltaic (CPV) modules operating at 10 to 20 suns (20 times normal sun intensity). This could yield up to 20 times more power than a standard PV module. The static module will use a novel sunlight trapping and focusing to maximise cell light capture and will avoid costly tracking and cooling systems. These modules could be used as remote off-grid power generation sources or for grid connected power.

This project will develop new R&D technology for sustainable energy generation in collaboration with advanced manufacturing companies in the local area.

p-2011-232 – An investigation into the effects of meshing around regions of stress concentration

Set by Dr Stephen Hardy ( s.j.hardy@swansea.ac.uk ), Civil and Computational Engineering Centre ( C2EC ).

Available for selection in next round? No

Description

Finite element stress analysis is an extremely powerful computation method for predicting displacements, stresses and strains in components and structures subject to tension, compression, bending, torsion, shear and pressure and, in many cases, combinations of two or more of these fundamental loading types.

It is an approximate method and so the accuracy of the predictions depends, to a great extent, on the model. Stress concentrations can be produced by real effects such as holes, fillets etc. or unrealistic effects such as point loads. In the latter case, the predictions can be highly unrealistic and methods to take account of these high predicted stresses have been proposed.

Conjugated polymers are relatively new materials that consist of alternating double & single carbon-carbon bonds along the backbone of the polymer. Laser is now available for cutting and welding large plates and is becoming the only tool to cut new generation of steels. Laser performance and steel development are therefore strictly related. New steels aim stronger and lighter components improving carbon emission footprint. The project should aim to define the state of the art of Laser cutting/welding and foresee the near future. This is a critical issue for the UK manufacturing industry. Student should find out information trough literature research as well as by contacting major European Steel and Laser companies. An understanding of material properties (interaction with light, cutting welding) and Matlab skills are essential.

Associated with: Atkins Carbon Critical (C2)

Cross disciplinary research theme(s): Energy and environment

Special requirements
